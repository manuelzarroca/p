Nomadful

Being a free alternative to mainstream mental health apps, Nomadful's goal is to combine accessible design with handy features to offer a useful solution that helps users focus and relax, understand their feelings, and get the right help whenever needed.

Introduction

[Personal comment]: As a young adult, I think people my age in general often go through stuff. I wish I had some kind of mental health friend to talk to about my feelings, when I had arguments at school with my mates, or when I was just feeling lonely and blue. Plus, I think we should all agree that mental health is vital regardless of age.

With prices for health services skyrocketing, it's becoming increasingly difficult for people to afford a psychologist visit, so humans end up relying on mental health and meditation apps. However, since most of these apps are unregulated, people end up paying extremely expensive subscriptions.

So here's where Nomadful steps in. It's an app aiming to solve the social injustice behind access to mental health services. The app offers users features like breathing sessions, soundscapes, mood logging and a journal. All of these features are made available to users completely free of charge, and the app monetises using native advertisements on a feed that contains guides on meditation, health and even tourism, as well as recipes and workouts.

The entire app's content was created by me, from guides to sounds, with the help of professional collaborators including psychologists working at my high school, who made suggestions and helped shape some of the app's most important features, like the Help Portal, a feature that comes handy for users seeking mental help in over 40 major regions, as it displays useful contact information from both NGOs and government departments offering free mental health counseling in each region. All of the services offered in the portal were carefully reviewed to guarantee that they can help users out. Additionally, I incorporated a company in the United Kingdom to operate and publish my app under a higher trust level.

Nomadful sets a new standard for accessibility thanks to its availability in over 30 languages, 5 of which are minority languages: Maori, Northern Sami, Welsh, Yucatec Maya and Zulu. Its intuitive and user-friendly interface uses a dark colour palette by default to save device battery.

Creative process

For the design and development stages of this project I used Figma, GitHub, Visual Studio, Xcode, and Android Studio.

Most of the design happened paralel to the app's development. This method allows me to make accurate choices and create executable elements that work as intended from the beginning. It is a computational approach to design that I implement in all of my projects.




The making of Nomadful consisted of the following 4 stages:

Research and initial planning
[Personal comment]: I learned valuable information on how mental health organisations work across different regions and the services they offer. I even came across some very interesting ones, such as Greenland's Tusaannga, a service offering mental health counselling to children and youth, where, if underage, they can find a Najorti, an adult that can help them through difficult times.

I also discovered similarities and differences between languages, and had plenty of time to connect with their diversity and read more on them (which is something I'm very keen on).

Many of the problems Nomadful aimed to solve were visible: talking about mental health and feelings was seen as negative, wellness apps offering pricy features that, honestly, we could do for free, and a lack of accessibility in products that were already on that market. There was a big window of opportunity.

Design system
Preparing a design system before starting with Nomadful's development was key to saving resources (time, money, energy) and having a functional prototype ready faster. Creating a design system involved selecting multiple elements, such as colours, typefaces, layouts and transitions. A logo and an app icon were also created.

The colour palette is made up of four colours: white, light grey, ocean dust and Nomadful blue as the accent colour. The app icon's default edition uses a linear gradient. A pride app icon was also created.






Having accessibility, language and authenticity in mind, two typefaces were picked for the app: Spline Sans as main typeface, and Phudu, for titles on the website, advertisements and other graphics. Spline Sans resembles a standard sans typeface, with modern and formal touches. It has a comprehensive charset for the latin alphabet and therefore is a good match for a mental health app whose goal is to be available to as many people as possible.


Phudu and Spline Sans on the app's website


Spline Sans charset diversity


The app's layout is simple. A three-tab navigation system allows the user to access the desired section with just one or two taps. In the home tab, an action button for each main feature (breathing, mood logs and journal) within thumb reach helps the user quickly start a breathing session, log their current mood or write their feelings down on a new journal entry.




Transitions are simple and straightforward, and were created entirely using CSS and JavaScript. They're easy on the eyes and comply with accessibility practices. They are mainly soft opacity and hierarchy animations. By controling these properties instead of each element's initial display state, loading times were optimised and as a result the overall app experience felt faster and smoother.

App development
The app was built multiplatform-ready from the beginning using web-based technologies and Visual Studio. Afterwards, the app was compiled and bundled both in Xcode, for iOS, and in Android Studio, for a planned Android version, using a native webview element with extended capabilities and permissions (device storage, camera, notifications).

App publishing

To be able to submit my app to the App Store, I registered a company in the UK and set up a guardian who was of legal age. After months of back and forth email conversations, my app was finally available on November 18th, 2024.

The app publishing process required patience, research and communication. By registering a company, I was able to operate the app under a more formal image and build further trust with users. This stage gave me advanced knowledge on the legal and technical challenges and requirements businesses go through every day.

Feature spotlight
Nomadful ID: a pocket-size digital ID that helps you access health apps and services, event venues and more

Nomadful ID was part of one of the app's latest updates. It's a digital ID that contains your medical record, vitals, and other important information, allowing you to fill out hospital registration forms, securely share that same data with third-parties on health apps like insurance portals, verify your age to access events, and even share your allergies with restaurant kiosks for a personalized dining experience.



Third-party website login with Nomadful ID


The data from Nomadful ID is encrypted and stored locally on the user's device, ensuring they have full control over what they share and who they share it with. Data can only be shared when the user decides to show their scannable code. Using a checkbox interface, the user can check and uncheck what they want and don't want to share depending on the context.
For example, when verifying your age to access an event venue, why should sensitive data like your name, date of birth or blood type be visible?


Sample Nomadful ID and checkbox interface

Left: age verification for concert tickets / Right: allergy selection


Additionally, I created a companion scanner app for third-party organisations interested in accessing Nomadful ID data. To comply with privacy, I implemented a timer that erases a user's Nomadful ID data after 20 seconds, so organisations can only inspect it once per session. To allow for actual data readability, organisations can set up parameters for faster and easier inspection.
For example, if a restaurant is looking for a specific allergy, they can identify it within seconds. Parameters that organisations can set up include region, blood type, sex, and allergies.

Scanner app using age verification parameter


As for third-party apps, websites and digital services, I created a specific JavaScript library for Nomadful, called nomadful.js. Using just a couple lines of code, third-parties can implement a login system that allows users to share their Nomadful ID data. It works by just loading the script, adding an HTML element, and specifying a callback URL for redirection once the data transfer process is completed successfully. The Nomadful app acts as a mediator between the user and the target service, adding privacy and security controls, and allowing data transfer only with the user's conscent.

Closure

Nomadful has been my best project so far. I poured my heart into it and not only acquired new knowledge and remastered my skills, but got the chance to challenge myself and contribute to positive change through design and development, which I'll always be thankful for. This project trully gave me something new to learn every day. There's never been a better feeling than to know that something you've done is out there helping others in unimaginable ways.

Nomadful was a bootstrapped project. In late 2025, I dediced to focus on my personal goals: getting accepted into university, building meaningful relationships and reconnecting with myself. I determined it was best to sunset the project.

Thanks for taking the time to read all the way down here.
